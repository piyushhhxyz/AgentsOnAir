# Proven Cold Outreach Templates

## 1. The "Mutual Connection" Template

**When to use:** You share a connection, community, or event with the recipient. Works best when the connection is genuine and recent.

**Expected response rate:** 18-25% (highest of all cold templates)

**Template:**
> Subject: [Mutual connection] suggested I reach out
>
> Hi [First Name],
>
> [Connection name] mentioned you're tackling [specific challenge] at [Company]. We helped [similar company] solve the same problem — they cut [metric] by [X%] in [timeframe].
>
> Would it be worth a 15-minute call to see if we could do the same for your team?
>
> Best,
> [Your name]

**Why it works:** Social proof + specificity. The mutual connection provides implicit trust, and the concrete result gives a reason to engage.

## 2. The "Specific Observation" Template

**When to use:** You've noticed something specific about their company — a product launch, job posting, funding round, or public challenge.

**Expected response rate:** 14-20%

**Template:**
> Subject: Noticed [specific observation] — quick thought
>
> Hi [First Name],
>
> Saw that [Company] just [specific observation — e.g., "launched your new API platform" / "posted 5 DevOps roles" / "closed your Series B"]. Congrats!
>
> That usually means [inference about their need — e.g., "scaling infrastructure is top of mind"]. We help teams at that stage [specific value prop — e.g., "reduce deployment time by 60%"].
>
> Happy to share how [similar company] handled it — no pitch, just the playbook. Worth 10 minutes?
>
> [Your name]

**Why it works:** Demonstrates you've done homework. The observation-to-inference chain shows you understand their context, not just their company name.

## 3. The "Value-First" Template

**When to use:** You have a genuinely useful resource (report, benchmark, tool, insight) relevant to their role or industry.

**Expected response rate:** 12-18%

**Template:**
> Subject: [Resource] for [their specific context]
>
> Hi [First Name],
>
> We just published a [resource type — e.g., "benchmark report on AI agent costs across 200 companies"]. Given [Company]'s work in [their space], thought you'd find the [specific section] useful.
>
> Here's the link: [URL]
>
> No strings attached — just thought it'd be relevant. Happy to discuss any of the findings if you're curious.
>
> [Your name]

**Why it works:** Leads with generosity. Reciprocity bias means people who receive value feel compelled to engage. The key is the resource must be genuinely useful, not a thinly disguised pitch.

## 4. The "Quick Question" Template

**When to use:** When you want to start a dialogue rather than pitch directly. Best for senior decision-makers who ignore overt sales messages.

**Expected response rate:** 10-16% (but leads to higher-quality conversations)

**Template:**
> Subject: Quick question about [their area of expertise]
>
> Hi [First Name],
>
> I'm researching how [role type — e.g., "engineering leaders at Series B companies"] are handling [specific challenge]. [Company] seems to be doing this well.
>
> Curious: are you [specific question — e.g., "building vs buying your internal tooling for this"] or taking a different approach?
>
> Either way, I'll share what I'm finding across the industry — some surprising patterns emerging.
>
> Thanks,
> [Your name]

**Why it works:** Flattery (subtle) + curiosity gap. Asking for their expertise signals respect. The promise to share findings creates a value exchange.

## Key Principles Across All Templates

- **Personalize the first line** — generic openers kill response rates
- **Keep under 100 words** — every word must earn its place
- **One clear CTA** — don't give multiple options
- **Sound like a human** — read it aloud; if it sounds robotic, rewrite
- **Test subject lines** — they determine whether your email gets opened at all
